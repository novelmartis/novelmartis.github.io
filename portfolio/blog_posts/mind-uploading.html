A few thoughts on Mind Uploading

Essay / 2022

A skeptical-but-curious take on identity, continuity, substrate, and what it would mean for an upload to really count as the same person.

Slides

Mind uploading is often framed as a futuristic transfer problem: move a person from biology to another substrate and keep the person intact. But that framing hides the hardest question. What exactly has to be preserved for the result to count as you rather than a merely convincing imitation?

Identity Approximation

One way to think about the problem is to treat a mind as a trajectory through a space of mind-states rather than as a frozen object. Some parts of that trajectory change quickly, such as momentary experiences or current perceptions. Other parts change slowly, such as personality, moral commitments, long-term memories, and habitual ways of evaluating the world.

If an upload had to preserve literally every aspect of a person, the goal would become paralyzing. A more workable view is that identity is approximate and structured: some components matter more than others, and some losses may be tolerable while others are not. The hard part is deciding which distortions still preserve the person and which ones break continuity.

Identity Emulation

Humans already do something like weak identity emulation all the time. We build internal models of other people, predict what they will think, and sometimes even inhabit their point of view closely enough for empathy, imitation, or performance. Acting is perhaps the most vivid case: the better the internalized model, the more convincingly a person can inhabit another identity.

That does not solve uploading, but it suggests a route. If identities can be approximated, documented, and partially instantiated in another cognitive system, then the question becomes what kind of substrate could sustain that approximation as an ongoing, learning, self-updating person rather than a static impersonation.

Substrate Requirements

An upload-worthy substrate would need to support rich perception, memory, learning, action, and self-maintenance. It is not enough to generate plausible sentences or replay stored facts. The system would need to persist, learn from new experiences, adapt over time, and keep the structure that makes those experiences belong to one continuing entity.

Current AI systems are still far from that. Even strong systems remain narrow, brittle, or weak on long-term continuity. So the practical question may not be how to upload a mind tomorrow, but how to think clearly about what kinds of preservation, approximation, and embodiment would be necessary for such a thing to ever make sense.

The Long Road

There is also a quieter, more immediate version of the problem: how should identities be preserved now, while no true upload substrate exists? Biographies, records, memories, correspondence, and digital traces all preserve fragments of a person. Perhaps the path toward any future transfer begins not with copying brains, but with learning what information actually matters for reconstructing an identity well enough to carry forward.
