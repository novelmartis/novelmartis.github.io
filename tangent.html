What is this?

Tangent is a small-group mentorship program for curious students in grades 8–10. Each cohort — just three students — works through a serious text together over four weeks: one 1.5-hour session per week, with guided reading in between.

The goal is not to finish a syllabus. It is to get students started on how to read hard material on their own, ask sharp questions, and use AI as a thinking partner — not a shortcut.

Tracks

Physics
The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. 1

Start from atoms in motion. Work toward gravity, probability, and the edges of what classical physics can explain. No calculus required — just the willingness to follow an argument.

Mathematics
Proofs from the Book — Aigner & Ziegler

Six proofs that primes are infinite. The irrationality of √2, e, and π. The Pigeonhole Principle. Euler's formula for polyhedra. Each chapter is a small miracle — a surprising result proved in a way that seems, in retrospect, inevitable.

AI & Cognitive Neuroscience
Vehicles — Valentino Braitenberg

Fourteen imaginary machines of increasing complexity. Vehicle 1 has two wires. Vehicle 14 has something that looks uncomfortably like a personality. Nothing magical happens in between — and that's the point. When does mechanism become mind? This track is led by an active researcher in the field.

About the mentor

Dr. Sushrut Thorat is a researcher in machine learning and cognitive neuroscience. His work studies how the brain represents the visual world, and how artificial neural networks can be made to better reflect biological vision.

He has supervised 20+ students across undergraduate, masters, and PhD levels, and has run reading groups for years — at university and informally with students in India. Several of those informal mentees went on to IIT or master's programmes in Europe.

Currently based in India; moving to Sydney (MARCS Institute, Western Sydney University) in 2026. Sessions are online.

Express interest

Takes 15–20 minutes to fill out honestly. No right answers — just be specific.

Be specific. "I read about physics" is not an answer. "I watched a video about why a gyroscope doesn't fall over and then spent an hour reading about angular momentum" is.
Not "I want to understand everything about the universe." Something specific: a question you've had, a thing that confused you, a result that didn't make sense.
"I gave up" is fine to say. "I haven't tried" is fine to say. I want to know how you handle difficulty.
Not "I want to get into IIT." Why does any of this matter to you, even a little?
Doesn't have to be about academics.

For parents / guardians

What happens after you send this

I read every form. If it seems like a good fit, I'll reach out within a week to set up a short call — 15 minutes, mostly so you can ask me questions and I can get a sense of what the student is curious about in conversation.

If I think it's not the right fit right now, I'll say so honestly.

Cohorts form when I have three students with overlapping interests. The wait is usually a few weeks. Fees are discussed on the call — the first cohort is free.
